# Commodity Catalog of Renaissance Venice

This document provides a comprehensive catalog of tradable goods in Renaissance Venice, organized by category and including key information for market analysis and trading decisions.

## Raw Materials

### Metals
- **Gold**
  - **Sources**: Hungary, Africa
  - **Uses**: Jewelry, decoration, currency, gilding
  - **Market Characteristics**: High value, stable demand, government-regulated
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, high security requirements
  - **Trading Units**: Ounces, small ingots

- **Silver**
  - **Sources**: Central Europe, Balkans
  - **Uses**: Coinage, tableware, jewelry, mirrors
  - **Market Characteristics**: Monetary metal, price linked to currency values
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, security requirements
  - **Trading Units**: Ounces, ingots, worked pieces by weight

- **Copper**
  - **Sources**: Alps, Hungary, Sweden
  - **Uses**: Cookware, decorative items, bronze production, roofing
  - **Market Characteristics**: Industrial metal, steady demand
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, oxidizes if not protected
  - **Trading Units**: Ingots, sheets, bars

- **Tin**
  - **Sources**: England, Bohemia
  - **Uses**: Bronze production, pewter, food containers
  - **Market Characteristics**: Essential industrial metal, limited sources
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, low melting point
  - **Trading Units**: Ingots, bars

- **Iron**
  - **Sources**: Alps, Germany, Sweden
  - **Uses**: Tools, weapons, construction, ship fittings
  - **Market Characteristics**: Fundamental industrial metal
  - **Storage Properties**: Rusts if not protected, heavy
  - **Trading Units**: Bars, ingots, pig iron

- **Lead**
  - **Sources**: England, Germany, Spain
  - **Uses**: Roofing, pipes, type metal, weights
  - **Market Characteristics**: Utilitarian metal, steady demand
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, toxic, heavy
  - **Trading Units**: Ingots, sheets, pigs

### Textiles (Raw)
- **Raw Silk**
  - **Sources**: China, Persia, Italy (later)
  - **Uses**: Luxury fabric production
  - **Market Characteristics**: High value, luxury material
  - **Storage Properties**: Requires dry conditions, vulnerable to pests
  - **Trading Units**: Skeins, bundles

- **Cotton**
  - **Sources**: Egypt, Levant, later New World
  - **Uses**: Fabric production, padding, lamp wicks
  - **Market Characteristics**: Growing demand, imported material
  - **Storage Properties**: Requires dry storage, flammable
  - **Trading Units**: Bales

- **Wool**
  - **Sources**: England, Spain, Italian mainland
  - **Uses**: Fabric production, felt making
  - **Market Characteristics**: Staple material, quality varies by source
  - **Storage Properties**: Must be kept dry, vulnerable to moths
  - **Trading Units**: Sacks, bales

- **Flax**
  - **Sources**: Northern Europe, Italian mainland
  - **Uses**: Linen production, rope making
  - **Market Characteristics**: Utilitarian material, domestic and imported
  - **Storage Properties**: Must be processed and stored properly
  - **Trading Units**: Bundles

### Building Materials
- **Marble**
  - **Sources**: Carrara, Greece, Istria

  - **Market Characteristics**: Luxury building material, highly variable by quality
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, heavy, requires careful handling
  - **Trading Units**: Blocks, slabs

- **Stone**
  - **Sources**: Istria, mainland Italy
  - **Uses**: Construction, paving
  - **Market Characteristics**: Essential building material
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, very heavy
  - **Trading Units**: Blocks, cut pieces

- **Timber**
  - **Sources**: Alpine regions, Dalmatian coast
  - **Uses**: Construction, shipbuilding, furniture
  - **Market Characteristics**: Essential material, subject to depletion concerns
  - **Storage Properties**: Must be seasoned properly, fire risk
  - **Trading Units**: Logs, boards, beams

- **Brick**
  - **Sources**: Local production, terraferma
  - **Uses**: Construction
  - **Market Characteristics**: Basic building material
  - **Storage Properties**: Non-perishable, breakable
  - **Trading Units**: Thousands, cartloads

  - **Trading Units**: Logs, chips, ground powder

## Production Chains

### Glass Production Chain
1. **Raw Materials**:
   - Silica sand (local lagoon)
   - Soda ash (imported from Syria/Egypt)
   - Limestone (mainland quarries)
   - Colorants (various sources)

2. **Processing Stages**:
   - Material preparation and mixing
   - Melting in specialized furnaces
   - Forming (blowing, casting, lampworking)
   - Finishing (cutting, grinding, polishing)
   - Decorating (gold leaf, enamel)

3. **Final Products**:
   - Luxury tableware
   - Mirrors
   - Beads
   - Windows
   - Decorative objects

### Textile Production Chain
1. **Raw Materials**:
   - Raw silk (imported from Asia, later Italy)
   - Cotton (imported from Egypt, Syria)
   - Wool (imported from England, Spain)
   - Flax (Northern Europe)
   - Dyes (various sources)

2. **Processing Stages**:
   - Fiber preparation (cleaning, combing)
   - Spinning into thread
   - Weaving
   - Dyeing (may occur at different stages)
   - Finishing treatments

3. **Final Products**:
   - Luxury fabrics (silk velvet, brocade)
   - Clothing
   - Household textiles
   - Ecclesiastical vestments
   - Flags and banners

### Shipbuilding Production Chain
1. **Raw Materials**:
   - Timber (various types from different sources)
   - Metals for fittings
   - Pitch and tar
   - Hemp for ropes
   - Canvas for sails

2. **Processing Stages**:
   - Timber seasoning and shaping
   - Hull construction
   - Interior fitting
   - Rigging installation
   - Finishing and waterproofing

3. **Final Products**:
   - Galleys (war vessels)
   - Round ships (merchant vessels)
   - Specialized vessels
   - Gondolas
   - Ship components

### Book Production Chain
1. **Raw Materials**:
   - Paper or parchment
   - Ink
   - Binding materials (leather, thread)
   - Decorative elements (gold leaf)

2. **Processing Stages**:
   - Manuscript writing or type setting
   - Printing
   - Illustration addition
   - Page assembly
   - Binding

3. **Final Products**:
   - Books (religious, classical, scientific)
   - Pamphlets
   - Maps
   - Government documents
   - Commercial records

## Market Relationships

### Complementary Goods
- **Ships and Naval Stores**: Increased shipbuilding drives demand for pitch, hemp, timber
- **Textiles and Dyes**: Textile production increases demand for dyestuffs
- **Luxury Goods and Packaging**: Fine products require quality containers and presentation
- **Books and Paper**: Printing industry drives paper demand

### Substitute Goods
- **Different Textile Fibers**: Wool, cotton, silk, linen can substitute based on price and availability
- **Lighting Sources**: Olive oil, tallow, beeswax compete for lighting applications
- **Building Materials**: Stone, brick, timber offer alternative construction options
- **Writing Surfaces**: Paper, parchment, vellum serve similar purposes at different price points

### Derived Demand
- **Glass Production**: Creates demand for soda ash, silica, colorants
- **Shipping Industry**: Drives demand for timber, canvas, rope
- **Printing Industry**: Creates demand for paper, ink, binding materials
- **Construction**: Drives demand for stone, timber, metals

## Implementation Guidelines

When working with commodities:

1. **Consider the Full Value Chain**
   - Trace commodities from raw materials through processing to final goods
   - Identify bottlenecks where controlling one resource affects multiple products
   - Recognize value-adding opportunities at each production stage

2. **Analyze Market Connections**
   - Identify complementary goods where demand moves together
   - Recognize substitute products that compete for market share
   - Understand how political or environmental events affect different commodity groups

3. **Factor in Practical Considerations**
   - Account for storage requirements and perishability
   - Consider transportation challenges for heavy or fragile goods
   - Recognize seasonal availability and price fluctuations

4. **Balance Portfolio by Commodity Type**
   - Diversify across different commodity categories
   - Mix high-value luxury goods with stable necessity items
   - Balance perishable goods with durable commodities
   - Combine imported exotic items with locally produced goods
